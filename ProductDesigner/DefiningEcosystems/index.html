Uniting design systems

The Geotab suite has over 20 years of no product or design oversight. The result is a suite of complicated products that are extremely difficult to learn.

At this point in my tenure at Geotab, the essentials of our design system had been built, but the bones were still the same. A retrofit was in order.

Role

Design Lead | Project Manager


Company

Geotab


Duration

10 months

Background

Geotab is old for a tech business. Over 20 years of historic development, expansion, and versioning have built the product into what it is today. Design as a discipline has only been a recent integration, and the design system is even more recent.

While the consolidation of the 6 design systems was successful in making Geotab visually more cohesive, the products were still not easy to learn or even navigate. For more background see my uniting design systems project.

Process

Defining ecosystems

What are ecosystems?

A design system ecosystem is a set of components, documentation, and most importantly, principles, that represent a typical interaction task flow. Similar to templates, but not nearly as rigid, ecosystems give designers the groundwork to build off of, while not being limiting in edge cases.

Think of it like interior design, best practices, and tactics to lay out your home beautifully but generally not prescriptive and “cut-and-paste”.

Why ecosystems?

In a product and team at this scale, ecosystems can make a massive impact:

  • Validation for one, confidence for many: Less effort is required to focus research effort on well validated ecosystems.
  • Consistent interaction design: Interaction flows that follow ecosystems will become easier to learn with easy to follow expectations.
  • Improved feedback cycles and constraints: With consistent implementation, pattern improvements will happen organically with more validation.
  • New iterative design philosophy: Introducing a change to the company of steady feature and product improvement, versus rapid feature additions.

Why ecosystems for this project?

Aside from the above, the primary reason for implementing ecosystems was momentum. Geotab with its fresh coat of (design system) paint was already seeing the benefits of a single design system and there was a hunger for more.

Further over 90% of the actions users completed on Geotab could be distilled to half a dozen ecosystems. From these half a dozen ecosystems, less than 5 percent of users found any of them a positive experience in a UMUX study we ran.

Pattern anatomy

A pattern is made up of 3 core pieces:

  • Principles
    Think of these as the guidelines of working with a pattern. They should be defined in text, agnostic of visuals so they can applied in multiple contexts. To allow that flexibility, simplifying these principles and avoid prescribing mandatory requirements unless necessary
  • Components
    The visual makeup of a pattern, these are applied following the principles and documentation. Components can be shared and imported as a figma component library, but are not always required. These are often examples of ecosystems in action versus reusable elements.
  • Documentation
    The finer details for both development and design partners. Documentation details the exact specifics of implementation, typically seen in context of your project.

Concepting

Building a concept car

To serve as the “concept car” as my director defined, the team needed a high impact, low(er) effort pattern to start. Surfaced in my previous work in centralizing safety, Geotab’s methods of navigation were cumbersome for all users. The primary navigation in particular had 50+ links organized without any consistency.

Navigation across the product was Geotab’s number 1 most requested improvement from both resellers and customers. This made it the strongest candidate to serve as the proof of concept. A month-long initiative led by myself defined a new navigation structure and planet-to-city data org.

The result was a task-focused IA that improved UMUX across the product by 5% and increased page-to-page engagement by 18%. UMUX lite became the primary metric moving forward, with pattern-specific metrics applied where needed.

Planning

Determining process

As ecosystems require more abstract thinking than conventional design work, the process of defining one needed to change.

Originally taking the navigation pattern process as discrete steps, the process was broadened to answering questions in 3 phases that surround the typical double diamond. This made ecosystems more accessible across the team. Questions like:

  • Determine scope: What other parts of the product use this pattern? How can you measure the effectiveness of the pattern?
  • Document your decision making: Why did you choose to do x over y? Where were compromises made?
  • Validate & get feedback: How have users responded to the changes? What improvements can be made?

Building a backlog

With navigation pattern work, a product-wide audit was completed to find other interaction task flows that fell under the “pattern” definition. Translated to Jira, this served as the backlog for further pattern work.

Collaborating with design management and product, the list was prioritized from low to high impact. The top became the focus for the initial broader pattern roll-out.

Assembling a team

Utilizing the positive engagement results from the navigation pattern, a successful pitch for resourcing was made to the executive team, opening up a dedicated budget for the first phase of ecosystems.

Working with design management, a team was formed of 4 designers to be managed by myself and the indirect engagement of 2 designers in pattern-related projects. The team structure was based on the previously completed backlog and prioritization.

Project managing

Improving process

A trackable backlog became a priority with a first pass completed individually, before engaging the now-transitioning team for feedback improvements. Establishing how we function as a team became critical to ensure overlapping efforts are flagged, provide opportunities for feedback, and give team members the chance to collaborate.

Focusing efforts

The initial scope of work was ecosystems for data manipulation and data presentation. These were quite broad and agnostic of specific use cases (but having a few as examples for testing). This enabled a full rethink of prioritized ecosystems for both desktop and mobile such as tables, filtering, loading, and more.

As ecosystems were further refined, a space was needed to test the ecosystems for a broader roll-out. The asset page was chosen for its reliance on ecosystems for displaying and manipulating content.

Growing team skills

With the far more abstract nature of the pattern work versus conventional design work, some team members struggled. Combating this took a mixture of coaching, collaborative workshops, and importantly leveraging their skills.

On an individual level, 1 on 1’s helped to understand their goals, and how they could be layered into the pattern work.

Feature creep

Like any project of this scale on such a high-traffic page, every member of the product org wanted to layer in their features. It took extensive discussion and negotiation with team members to dictate what would be part of the MVP, what might be implemented in the future, and what would be avoided.

Pre-discussions, there were over 40 specific feature requests from the product org. Post, it had been reduced to 3.

Results

In data

The assets page was completed for the first phase of the project by development, with a smooth hand-off transition thanks to their early involvement in the process. The roll-out showed a promising improvement of 15% to UMUX on the assets page.

In testing, there was over a 50% lower failure rate on tasks and extremely positive feedback from participants. The improvements also were built to the AA web accessibility standards, an issue plaguing the company for years.

Learnings

  • Have a plan for resources: Having a discrete plan of where dedicated design and development resources would focus, as well as integrating pattern work where possible into pre-existing projects, made for a far easier pitch.
  • Alignment to avoid burnout: One of the largest challenges in the process was resourcing, with design, product, and development being pulled in different directions. The regular check-ins with teams alleviated this, as well as giving myself the opportunity to work with members to prioritize work, clarify direction, and help where necessary.
