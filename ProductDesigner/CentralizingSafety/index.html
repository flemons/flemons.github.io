Centralizing safety

Geotab’s bread and butter has always been in its robust data. OBD devices that can track anything from location to detailed engine telematics, and a historic data set of over 20 years, Geotab prized itself as being a one stop shop for everything fleet.

So why is so little of it used? Over 50% of our user base only utilizes location and routing data.

Role

Product design | UXR


Company

Geotab


Duration

6 months

Background

Data at Geotab was inaccessible to the typical user. Barring a technical API call, user have to surf for data scattered throughout the product in various states of raw form.

This came about from features being built indepdently of users actual in processes. Before design joining Geotab, instead of taking time to understand why a user had a need, they built to satisfy an RFP.

Process

Research

Audit current understanding

Time with customers is difficult to come by at Geotab, making identifying what knowledge already exists before planning critical. Between the internal research review and product audit, some business and user highlights included:

  • The disparity in through-ways: No easy way to move through the product without immense training and experience
  • Poor understanding of safety-focused users and tasks: Very little is understood beyond surface-level processes informed only by our large partners.
  • Driver/manager relationship: A lot of forum feedback from drivers and safety managers regarding their frustrating relationships with safety and cold feelings towards each other.

Plan and conduct research

With safety managers and drivers as the focus, developing an understanding of safety tasks, along with the drivers and managers impacted by them oriented the primary research.

The research initially entailed interview questions only. Eventually, a greater understanding of common tasks enabled the inclusion of some user task sharing, demonstrating how they use Geotab today. Some of the hypothesis that helped frame the research included:

  • Users have consistent, repeatable processes for safety tasks.
  • Drivers have a disdain for and do not get benefit of being monitored.
  • There is a lack of clear outcomes for drivers in following through with a safety journey.

Results

With a cohort of 7 safety managers and 3 drivers spanning large and small organizations, some insights entailed:

  • Drivers dont get value from their data
  • Safety is everyone's responsibility in the company
  • Safety managers have difficulty presenting safety relevant data in a concise way depending on the audience
  • Safety managers have an appetite for more data, but not time
  • Safety managers spend a large majority of the time answering “how is my fleet doing?”

Vision

Craft a data story

In all sizes of fleets, there is an insatiable appetite for more data but little time to interpret it. Especially in most small-medium business cases, the safety manager also manages numerous other aspects of the business (routing, sales, maintenance, etc.).

Collaborating with data science and using our safety journey map as a guide, 3 key steps were defined that help tell a story to customers instead of an information dump. Exploration into interpreted versus raw data yielded a simpler data set, enabling users “at a glance” to understand their fleet's safety.

Project roadmap

Using the information gathered from the data science team, as well as our development partners, creating a stepped roadmap helped define for executives the vision for “centers”.

Doing so also forced a revisit with product of the project's initial goal, consolidating data. This was the start of a product manager's involvement in this project.

UI design

Ideation

With the data story and journey map as a guide, ideation focused on what layouts best served customers' “at a glance” needs. An uncluttered top-to-bottom format was received best in ad-hoc internal testing.

As a first take on a center, this also served as an opportunity to define how we present data visually in a replicable way. The “data visualization container” was created as a design system component that best represented both the ideal state of data being presented, but also the limitations of the chart.js library the development team had opted to use.

Results

Reaction and effects

Originally released in beta, users engaged with more of the product than ever before. Engagement on through-way pages (that present more data related to safety) at a minimum increased by 200%..

From the user testing conducted 100% of the users claimed they would use the safety center in their day to day. In the 10,000+ user pool we released the beta too, we saw nearly a quarter of users who used the safety center return to it weekly.

These positive metrics have led to a roll-out of additional “centers” that follow the story defined in this first pass and a company shift from raw data density to interpreted data transparency.

The negative metric of low overall safety center engagement was caused by convoluted navigation, which catalyzed the design system pattern project.

Learnings

  • Have a plan for resources: Having a discrete plan of where dedicated design and development resources would focus, as well as integrating pattern work where possible into pre-existing projects, made for a far easier pitch.
  • Alignment to avoid burnout: One of the largest challenges in the process was resourcing, with design, product, and development being pulled in different directions. The regular check-ins with teams alleviated this, as well as giving myself the opportunity to work with members to prioritize work, clarify direction, and help where necessary.
