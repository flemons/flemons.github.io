Bonci - Find New Restaurants

Product Design
UI/UX Design

A better app for finding restaurants

How might I improve the restaurant finding experience?

Summary

This project saw the research and design of a competitive app in the restaurant finding space.

After personal experiences while travelling, I identified restaurant findings apps as a major market sector needing improvement.

The restaurant finding app sector at the time of the project was poor, with the majority using outdated app design. I researched and identified key aspects needed for an application in this space.

Problem

A buyout and subsequent cancellation of a major app in the restaurant reviewing field left a large untapped market.

Competitors in the field at the time left much to be desired. With common issues of low app functionality, too much and poorly laid out information, as well as frustrating navigation, a new take was needed.

Solution

A new application was designed to solve this problem. Bonci provides a simple, straightforward interface that encourages users to review more often through incentive.

Additionally, content limitation is key for the application, to ensure loading speed and memory use are kept to a minimum.

Process

Research

Summary

  • Interviewed 5 users
  • Users spend minimal time spent on this app type
  • Used it mainly for finding new restaurants, not going back to old
  • Used commonly while travelling
  • Almost all users do not leave reviews

Interviews

To get a better grasp on the problem area, and identify if it was purely personal or widespread, 5 separate interviews were conducted in varying age demographics. Although the group was from the same community (Burlington/Oakville area), the users shared similar frustrations that I held.

Design Requirements

From the 5 interviews, I framed questions in order to get majority quantitative data over qualitative. This was to allow for ease of identifying trends. However I also received some detailed qualitative feedback as well.

These interviews were then amalgamated into a chart where I could identify trends. The app type itself is used very minimally, meaning that the users interviewed did not use it beyond finding a restaurant.

These users used these apps only for finding new restaurants, especially when they were on the move. This meant that travelling was the most common situation where these users would use the app . Having said that, their was still a majority of users who used these apps in their local community as well.

The biggest finding from the interviews however, and probably most important, was how few users actually ever left reviews. The majority would use the app only to find restaurants as previously mentioned, this poses a problem for population of the application, and user engagement.

Personas

Given the data gathered and users interviewed, two personas were created. These personas helped to better identify with the key audience members, as well as providing guidelines for the key path scenario moving forward.

Wireframe Prototyping

Summary

  • Followed similar format of navigation apps
  • Map on top, list on bottom
  • Found issues with text sizing and element sizing
  • Narrowed content more to combat

Wireframes

In order to rapidly create multiple designs, I constructed multiple wireframes of the potential app. Given the completed pieces I selected the best ones to move forward with based on my findings.

The design followed similar design trends from the time. A map on the top with a list of the content on the bottom, very similar to the google maps of the time.

The biggest issue I found when designing however was information density. This was because of how many key aspects customers look for when they are considering a restaurant. To combat this I narrowed the content down to as little as possible, and used iconography where it would best fit.

Key-Path Scenario

Delving deeper into the design of the interface, a key-path scenario was decided upon to demonstrate the typical user journey, using one of the personas already provided. The key-path scenario provided much needed verification of the app’s functionality, as well as navigation in the app.

Completed Piece

Summary

  • Removed more unnecessary content
  • Limited menu choices
  • Allows users to directly compare restaurants for them
  • Can provide suggestions based on users eating habits
  • Provides Incentive to users to review, chance to win gift cards to restaurants they may like

Using the final key-path scenario as the template, the final mockup took into consideration the added visual weight of image based items. To mitigate this, any unnecessary content was further cut, or simplified in the piece. This included limiting menu choices, as initially the page had a wide variety of options to select.

The final app allows user to directly compare restaurants from one to another. Users in the interview component complained about the need to keep swapping when deciding from one restaurant to another, this function limits that to an easy to view chart.

Another suggestion as AI learning was becoming very popular at this time, was the app to suggest restaurants for them. Using their previously liked options, an algorithm could identify what cuisines best suited the persons tastes.

For the major issue of a lack of community support in leaving reviews, an incentive program was devised. Similar to Gas Buddy, users who review restaurants are entered into a draw that would occur weekly for a restaurant branch gift card. This concept has been applied to numerous app since this initial construction, demonstrating its proof of concept.
